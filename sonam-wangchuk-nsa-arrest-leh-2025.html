Beyond Kashmir: Sonam Wangchuk’s NSA Arrest Sparks Outrage in Leh (27 September 2025)

27 September 2025 Leh, Ladakh National / Politics

On 27 September 2025, Ladakh, often celebrated as India’s serene ‘cold desert,’ became the epicenter of a national crisis. Renowned educator and climate activist Sonam Wangchuk was arrested under the National Security Act (NSA), a move widely perceived as an attempt to suppress a historically peaceful movement for Ladakh’s statehood and inclusion under the Constitution’s Sixth Schedule. The detention has sparked outrage in Leh, leading to clashes, curfews, and a broader debate over dissent and constitutional rights in India.

The Roots of the Ladakh Movement

Known for his innovative educational initiatives, which inspired the Bollywood film 3 Idiots, and his environmental work, including the ice stupa project, Sonam Wangchuk has been a vocal advocate for Ladakh’s future. The region’s designation as a Union Territory in 2019, following the abrogation of Article 370, initially raised hopes. However, the lack of legislative autonomy soon fueled discontent. Local leaders, citing Ladakh’s unique Buddhist and Shia Muslim cultures, fragile ecology, and limited resources, have rallied around two key demands:

  • Full statehood: To establish an elected legislative assembly for local governance.
  • Sixth Schedule protections: To grant autonomous district councils, safeguarding land, resources, and cultural identity, as seen in parts of Northeast India.

Wangchuk’s hunger strikes, including his ‘climate fast’ and ‘constitutional fast’ in sub-zero temperatures, have drawn global attention, elevating Ladakh’s demands beyond a regional issue.

Escalation and Arrest Under NSA

The arrest of Wangchuk under the NSA, a law allowing detention without formal charges for up to 12 months, has been labeled a drastic and controversial step. Authorities claim his speeches incited violence during protests, a charge Wangchuk and his supporters vehemently deny. Leh Police, backed by the Union Home Ministry, allege that Wangchuk’s actions disrupted public order, justifying the use of the NSA. His legal team, however, calls the arrest “political retaliation,” arguing that his advocacy for constitutional rights and non-violent protests does not constitute a national security threat.

Unrest in Leh: Clashes and Curfews

Following Wangchuk’s arrest, Leh witnessed widespread unrest. Defying curfews, large groups, particularly youths, took to the streets in the old town, chanting “Jai Ladakh” and demanding Wangchuk’s release. Eyewitnesses report attempts to break police barricades, met with baton charges and tear gas. Unverified social media footage shows security forces dispersing crowds, escalating tensions. Police confirmed eight additional arrests for alleged violence and arson, signaling a crackdown on movement leaders.

National and International Reactions

The arrest has triggered a political storm. Opposition leaders, including Shiv Sena (UBT) chief Uddhav Thackeray, condemned the government, calling the NSA’s use against a “patriot” an attack on democratic values. International human rights groups, such as Amnesty International, have urged India to cease using harsh laws like the NSA against peaceful dissenters, labeling Wangchuk’s detention “unlawful.” Local religious and social leaders in Ladakh warned that continued suppression could destabilize the region’s fragile peace.

The Road Ahead: A Test for Indian Democracy

Ladakh’s unrest poses a complex challenge for India, balancing geopolitical tensions along the Line of Actual Control (LAC) with China and a growing indigenous movement for constitutional rights. Wangchuk’s arrest has amplified the movement’s visibility, transforming it into a broader struggle for federalism, tribal autonomy, and the right to dissent. The government faces a critical choice: continue its crackdown, risking further unrest, or engage in dialogue with movement leaders. History suggests that suppression in mountainous regions often fuels resistance, positioning Ladakh as a testing ground for India’s democratic resilience.
