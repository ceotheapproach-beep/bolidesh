Curfew Imposed in Leh: Protests Over Ladakh Statehood and Sixth Schedule Demand (26 September 2025)

26 September 2025 Leh, Ladakh National / Politics

Alongside the arrest of climate activist Sonam Wangchuk, one of the most significant national developments on September 26, 2025 was the imposition of a widespread curfew in Leh (Ladakh). The curfew followed protests demanding full statehood for Ladakh and inclusion under the Sixth Schedule of the Constitution that turned violent. The measure underlines the extreme level of unrest in the Union Territory.

Immediate Causes of the Curfew

The announcement of the curfew in Leh came directly after violent clashes on the evening of September 24, 2025, reflecting a growing rift between Ladakh’s residents and the administration.

1. Violent Clashes of September 24

Protests that began peacefully over constitutional safeguards and local demands suddenly took a violent turn. A significant number of Gen Z youth were involved in confrontations with security forces. Protesters allegedly targeted government property, including an attempt to set fire to the ruling party’s office and torching CRPF vehicles. Police used lathi-charge, tear gas and ultimately resorted to firing to control the crowd. Official reports confirmed four deaths and nearly 80 injuries, including several security personnel. Authorities described the violence as “pre-planned” and a serious threat to law and order.

2. Arrest of Sonam Wangchuk

Renowned environmentalist Sonam Wangchuk was arrested on September 26 under the National Security Act (NSA). Officials accused him of inciting violence and making provocative statements. His detention further inflamed public anger, prompting the administration to strictly enforce curfew measures to prevent mass gatherings or retaliatory demonstrations.

Scope of the Curfew and Administrative Measures

The Leh district administration imposed stringent restrictions that effectively brought normal life to a halt in many parts of the city.

Nature of Restrictions

  • Comprehensive Curfew: Section 144 was enforced across much of Leh district, banning gatherings of four or more people. Residents were ordered to stay indoors except for essential activities.
  • Internet and Communication Blockade: Internet services in sensitive areas were restricted or suspended to curb rumors and misinformation.
  • Closure of Educational Institutions: All government and private schools, colleges, and universities were indefinitely closed.
  • Deployment of Additional Forces: Alongside Ladakh Police, large contingents of CRPF and ITBP were deployed; sensitive sites, government buildings and major junctions were placed under heavy surveillance.
  • Partial Relaxation: Some areas saw limited, time-bound relaxations for procuring essential supplies, while overall security remained tight.

Administrative Rationale

The Director General of Police (DGP) for Ladakh said the curfew aimed to restore public peace and order and was necessary to prevent “anti-social elements” from causing further unrest. He added that patrols had been heightened along Ladakh’s borders with China and Pakistan to ensure that external forces could not exploit the internal disturbance.

Roots of Ladakh’s Autonomy Demands

The curfew reflects deep dissatisfaction that has simmered since Ladakh was made a Union Territory in August 2019. Initially welcomed by many, the change soon revealed a key concern: Ladakh is administered without an elected assembly, leaving locals politically disempowered.

Residents cite environmental and land-security worries given Ladakh’s fragile ecology. They fear that an influx of outsiders could strain limited land and resources (including glaciers and water) and threaten their distinct Buddhist and Muslim cultural identity. Local representative bodies such as the Leh Apex Body (LAB) and the Kargil Democratic Alliance (KDA) say the central government has not adequately addressed their demands, offering only assurances instead of concrete action.

National and Political Implications

The curfew in Leh has once again brought Ladakh’s struggle to the national forefront:

  • Questions on Democracy: Opposition parties and human rights groups described the curfew and Wangchuk’s arrest as “undemocratic,” accusing authorities of using excessive force to stifle peaceful protest.
  • Challenge for the Centre: The unrest poses a major test for the Union Home Ministry. As a strategically vital border region, internal instability in Ladakh raises national security concerns. There is mounting pressure on the government to open meaningful dialogue with LAB and KDA.
  • Movement’s Momentum: Despite restrictions, Ladakhis remain resolute. Hashtags such as #SaveLadakh continued to trend on social media, indicating that public anger has not subsided.

Conclusion

The curfew imposed in Leh on September 26, 2025 was more than an administrative step — it symbolized a long-standing struggle for identity, land and ecological protection. It sent a clear message to the central government: delaying political and constitutional solutions risks fueling unrest in border regions. Even after the curfew is lifted, trust-building and the provision of constitutional safeguards are likely the only sustainable remedies. The pressing question remains: will the government take Ladakh’s demands seriously and move toward Sixth Schedule protections, or will this deadlock persist?

Note: This article is an English translation and summary of events reported around September 24–26, 2025. For ongoing developments, consult primary reporting from major outlets and official statements.
