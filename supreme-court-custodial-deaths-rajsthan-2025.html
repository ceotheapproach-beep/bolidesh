Human Rights Crisis: Supreme Court Takes Unprecedented Suo Motu Cognizance of Custodial Deaths in Rajasthan (27 September 2025)

27 September 2025 New Delhi, India National / Politics

The Supreme Court of India has taken suo motu cognizance of the alarming rise in custodial deaths in Rajasthan, expressing grave concern over 11 such incidents in the first eight months of 2025, with seven occurring in Udaipur alone. 0 1 2 3 In a landmark hearing, the court has directed the state government to submit detailed reports, involved the National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) for independent probes, and emphasized the need for comprehensive guidelines to protect detainees' rights. 0 4 This judicial intervention marks a stern rebuke to systemic police excesses and a zero-tolerance stance on human rights violations under the guise of law enforcement. 5

The Scale of the Crisis: Rajasthan's Figures and National Shame

Custodial deaths signal a profound human rights crisis in any democracy, and Rajasthan's statistics are particularly disturbing. 1 In the first eight months of 2025, 11 deaths were reported, elevating the state's monthly average to dangerous levels, with Udaipur accounting for seven—indicating a pattern of police brutality and administrative failure. 0 2 3 Police often cite 'suicide' or 'illness' as causes, but activists and families allege third-degree torture and inhumane conditions. 4 The court termed these 'constitutional failures,' stating: "Custodial death is the worst violation of Article 21 (right to life and liberty). Where police should protect, they are turning into killers, and this is unacceptable." 0

Special Focus on Udaipur: A Disturbing Pattern

Udaipur's seven deaths drew particular scrutiny, with three victims arrested for minor offenses like petty theft or public nuisance. 3 Reports suggest prolonged illegal detention, denial of access to lawyers or family, and brutal coercion for confessions. 2 Local magistrates failed to conduct timely inquiries or review custody conditions, prompting the court to remind them of their constitutional duties as guardians. 0

Supreme Court Directives and Scope of Inquiry

In its suo motu proceedings, the Supreme Court issued stringent directives for a thorough probe. 0 5

  • Immediate Reports: Rajasthan's Chief Secretary and DGP must submit detailed accounts of each death within three weeks, covering causes, circumstances, and police actions. 0
  • NHRC Involvement: NHRC to conduct independent investigations, ensuring criminal negligence cases against police proceed. 0
  • CCTV and Transparency: Compliance with 2020 orders for high-quality CCTVs in lock-ups and interrogation rooms; tampered footage will hold officers personally liable. 0 4
  • Magistrate Role: Magistrates must scrutinize medical exams and custody needs, especially with abuse complaints. 0

Global Implications: Need for Police Reforms in India

This issue transcends Rajasthan, highlighting national institutional failures and the urgent need for police reforms. 6

  • D.K. Basu Guidelines: The 1997 Supreme Court directives on arrests remain unimplemented, fueling abuse. 5
  • International Standards: India's unsigned UN Convention Against Torture draws global criticism on human rights forums. 4
  • Eroding Trust: Each death erodes public-police trust, underscoring the need for justice, fairness, and dignity in the criminal system. 3

Conclusion

The Supreme Court's suo motu action sends a powerful message that the judiciary remains the ultimate protector of human rights. 0 The probe's outcome could be a milestone for police and judicial reforms nationwide, compelling systemic changes to transform law enforcement from a tool of force to one of service. 7
