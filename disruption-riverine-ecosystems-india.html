Disruption of Riverine Ecosystems in India: The Multifaceted Crisis of Dams, Sand Mining, and Industrialization

September 28, 2025 Culture

Executive Summary

India’s riverine ecosystems face a crisis driven by illegal sand mining, dams, and untreated industrial/urban waste, threatening biodiversity, water quality, and livelihoods. These factors, compounded by organized crime and weak enforcement, disrupt river geomorphology and aquatic habitats. Key species like Mahseer and Gangetic dolphins are nearing extinction, while fishing communities lose their livelihoods. Solutions like manufactured sand (M-Sand) and community-driven eco-tourism, supported by stronger regulations, offer hope. The Bolidesh Foundation champions these efforts to preserve India’s environmental and cultural heritage.

Introduction

Riverine ecosystems, including wetlands and riparian zones, provide critical services like groundwater recharge, water purification, and flood mitigation. In India, these systems are under threat from sand mining, dams, and pollution, leading to irreversible ecological and socio-economic damage. This report analyzes the crisis in major river basins (Ganga, Narmada, Sabarmati, Gomti) and proposes solutions to restore resilience, aligning with Bolidesh Foundation’s mission to protect natural and cultural heritage.

Geomorphic Disruption

Illegal Sand Mining: Excessive sand extraction destabilizes riverbeds, causing channel deepening and bank erosion. In the Narmada basin, this destroys farmers’ seasonal crops and fish nurseries. Mafia-controlled mining, often backed by political patronage, defies National Green Tribunal (NGT) orders and PESA Act provisions, violating tribal rights in areas like Dhar and Barwani.

Dams and Barrages: Dams disrupt ecological flow (e-flow) and sediment transport, fragmenting aquatic connectivity. In the Narmada, multiple dams reduce downstream fertility and displace communities, exacerbating ecological damage.

Biodiversity Loss

Sand mining destroys fish nurseries, pushing species like Mahseer in the Narmada to near extinction. In the Ganga, dams and pollution threaten Gangetic dolphins, with physical barriers disrupting migration. Conservation efforts, like dolphin sanctuaries, are undermined by ongoing habitat destruction, highlighting the need for integrated protection strategies.

Water Quality Degradation

Untreated industrial and urban waste pollutes rivers, with Gomti’s 34-km stretch near Lucknow severely contaminated by paper industry effluents. Toxins like fluoride and arsenic risk groundwater contamination, creating a long-term environmental debt. Shrinking wetlands, critical for natural filtration, exacerbate flood risks and water quality issues due to unregulated tourism and encroachment.

Impact on Fishing Communities

Fishing communities, like those along the Narmada, face collapsing livelihoods due to declining fish stocks and riverbed agriculture losses. Displacement from mining and dam projects further threatens tribal communities, necessitating robust rehabilitation plans. Initiatives like the Ganga dolphin eco-tourism model empower locals, linking conservation with income generation.

Regulatory Framework and Failures

Despite robust laws like the EIA, NGT, and PESA Act, enforcement fails due to political interference and regulatory capture. Illegal mining persists despite judicial interventions, and PESA violations undermine tribal governance. The National Water Policy’s e-flow mandates are often ignored in dam operations, highlighting governance gaps.

Policy Recommendations

To restore riverine ecosystems, India should:

  • Mandate M-Sand Use: Phase in manufactured sand for construction to reduce river sand dependency, leveraging coal mine overburden.
  • Create Enforcement Task Forces: Form multi-agency units to combat sand mafia, ensuring officer safety and independence.
  • Enforce PESA Compliance: Mandate village council approvals for mining in PESA areas, empowering tribal communities.
  • Prioritize E-Flow: Make ecological flow non-negotiable in dam operations to restore aquatic connectivity.
  • Protect Wetlands: Allocate budgets for wetland restoration as critical climate adaptation infrastructure.
  • Integrate PMMSY with Conservation: Link fishing community livelihoods to eco-tourism and conservation under the PMMSY.

Conclusion

The disruption of India’s riverine ecosystems threatens biodiversity, water quality, and livelihoods, driven by sand mining, dams, and pollution. Weak enforcement and organized crime exacerbate the crisis. By adopting M-Sand, strengthening regulations, and empowering communities through eco-tourism, India can restore its rivers.
