This report presents an in-depth, authentic, and detailed analysis of the real economic impact of traditional fairs and festivals in India. This study covers all important aspects such as direct impact on micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs), labor market dynamics, sustainable capital formation, and policy efficiency deficits. This evaluation is especially based on comprehensive studies conducted by the Government of India's Ministry of Tourism in 36 states/union territories and the latest data from mega-events, to encompass the economic landscape even to the smallest areas of the country. 0

Section I: Broad Economic Context of Traditional Fairs and Introductory Analysis

1.1. Definition and Economic Classification of Indian Fairs: From National to Regional Level

The organization of fairs in India is not merely the fulfillment of religious or cultural traditions, but it acts as a powerful economic catalyst for rural and urban economies. These events cause a sudden increase in consumer spending and provide local vendors with a vast market platform, thereby enriching small businesses. From an economic perspective, Indian fairs can be classified into three broad categories based on their duration and geographical reach.

The first category includes 'mega events' such as the Kumbh Mela, which generate economic activity on an extraordinary scale and attract national and international attention. Government investment in these events reaches thousands of crores of rupees. The second category is 'fairs of national importance,' such as the Pushkar Fair, which are focused on specific trade sectors (such as livestock, handicrafts) and attract buyers and sellers from the entire state or region. The third and most widespread category is 'local/rural weekly haat markets' or small religious festivals. In-depth analysis reveals that even the foundation day of a small temple activates a complete service industry at the local level, including sound systems, lighting, event management teams, flower decorations, and catering services. This establishes the 'principle of micro-impact' that even in the smallest areas, these events have the potential for income generation and temporary employment.

1.2. Landscape of Government Assistance for Organizing Fairs (CFA and Initiatives of the Ministry of Tourism)

The Government of India recognizes the economic importance of these cultural events and provides Central Financial Assistance (CFA) to promote tourism flow. This assistance is provided annually by the Ministry of Tourism to state governments and union territory administrations.

To measure the success of this policy initiative, the Ministry of Tourism appointed Kantar IMRB (formerly IMRB International) to assess the impact of these fairs and festivals across the country. This study established the comprehensiveness of the economic footprint of Indian fairs. This extensive coverage included 81 different fairs and festivals spread across all 36 states and union territories of India. 0 This nationwide coverage ensures that the economic evaluation is not limited to major tourist destinations but also incorporates the economic stories of remote and small areas, in line with the user's request. During this study, interviews were conducted with more than 60,000 Indian and international tourists, including more than 24,400 listing interviews at their places of stay and more than 39,400 interviews at the fair site. 0 This depth of primary research establishes the authenticity of the report.

1.3. Objective of the Study and Criteria for Evaluating 'Real Impact'

The central objective of this report is to establish fairs not just as cultural celebrations but as 'engines of economic development.' For the evaluation of 'real impact,' complex criteria have been used instead of relying solely on direct sales figures. These criteria include direct revenue generation, the multiplier effect of consumer spending, sustainable asset creation (such as new roads and drainage), and most importantly, efficiency deficits like economic leakage. By integrating the latest, certified data for events like the Kumbh with the national findings of the Ministry of Tourism, this report serves as a policy-focused reference document. 0

1.4. System of Data Integration in the Report: Narrative Inclusion of Statistical Insights

In accordance with the user's strict demand, no tables have been included in this report. Therefore, all important statistical and comparative data have been presented in narrative prose in an extremely detailed and explanatory manner. For example, figures on tourist numbers, revenue estimates, and infrastructure investments have been carefully woven into the text, so that the significance of the numbers and their implications are clearly evident, but formal tabulation is avoided.

Section II: Direct Monetary Impact on Local Trade and Role of MSMEs

2.1. Detailed Analysis of Sales Growth for Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs): National Trends and Regional Variations

The most immediate and evident impact of fairs and festivals is the sudden increase in consumer spending for local MSMEs. These events provide a significant business opportunity for retail vendors, artisans, and small service providers, making a large portion of their annual income dependent on these seasonal events.

The volume of sales at the fair site can be assessed in the context of Maha Kumbh 2025. According to economic experts, more than 40,000 stalls are expected to operate during this event. There is wide variation in the daily sales of these stalls, but during peak weeks, they earn an average of ₹2,000 to ₹5,000 per day. Thus, during the entire duration of the event, these local vendors can collectively generate revenue between ₹560 crore and ₹1,400 crore. This figure shows that the fair is a powerful medium for direct revenue injection for local businesses. 10 Regional variations exist: while large religious fairs see high spending on religious artifacts and food, regional craft fairs provide a platform for the sale of handicrafts and handloom products, directly benefiting traditional artisans registered under schemes like SFURTI.

2.2. Structure of Consumer Spending: Breakdown of Expenditure Patterns of Visitors to Fairs

According to the Ministry of Tourism's study, analyzing visitors' spending patterns is important to understand their real impact on the local economy. The survey found that 39% of visitors were tourists who had come to the destination specifically to participate in the fair or festival. 0 These specific visitors are the group that generates the most revenue for the local economy, as their entire expenditure (accommodation, transportation, food, shopping) is driven by the fair.

The most important and often overlooked feature of this analysis is the pattern of visitors' accommodation. The surprising fact is that more than 65% of these fair-specific tourists prefer to stay in unregistered accommodations. These unregistered accommodations include staying at friends/family homes or using temporary camp sites. This trend indicates the existence of a vast, invisible "campsite economy." Since these visitors do not use the formal hospitality sector (registered hotels or guest houses), their spending on accommodation and associated services is not recorded in official tourism revenue data, making the real economic impact of fairs much higher than government estimates. This informalization allows local residents to earn income indirectly on one hand, but on the other hand, it also increases revenue leakage, which cannot be captured through the formal taxation system.

2.3. Impact on Artisans and Handicraft Industry: Evaluation of Revenue and Profit Margins

India's traditional fairs are the most important display and sales platforms for artisans and the handicraft industry. The contribution of this sector in mega-events like Maha Kumbh is unprecedented. For the Prayagraj Kumbh 2025, the estimated revenue from religious offerings and handicraft sales is ₹25,000 crore. 11 This figure shows that locally manufactured artifacts, worship materials, and souvenirs act as main pillars of the economy in religious-cultural fairs, directly supporting small artisans and local production units.

In addition, the impact of fairs is not limited to the duration of the event. The integration of technology has paved the way for long-term sales expansion for the handicraft industry. E-commerce platforms like Epicraft enable artisans from different regions of the country to market their unique and high-quality products to global audiences with a secure payment gateway. This digitalization model allows artisans to use the fair as a 'product launchpad,' after which they can continue their sales online. This approach reduces the income instability of traditional artisans, who are often overly dependent on seasonal events.

2.4. Changes in Regional Market Dynamics: Study of Commercial Changes in Animal Fairs (such as Pushkar)

The structure and success of traditional fairs are extremely sensitive to external regulatory and socio-political risks. The study of the prestigious annual animal fair held in Pushkar, Rajasthan, clearly highlights this contradiction.

The Pushkar Fair was once known for camel and cattle trade, but it has seen a significant structural change in the last decade. According to the latest data, the total animal sales in this year's fair are estimated to be around ₹11.5 crore. 19 High-value Marwari and Kathiawadi horses dominated this sale, reaching ₹10.3 crore, with a total trade of 883 horses.

In contrast, traditional livestock trade has been severely affected. Camel sales shrank to only 274 units, with a total value of ₹76 lakh, and this sale was entirely within Rajasthan. Animal Husbandry Department officials have clarified that camel sales have been almost negligible for the past decade, mainly due to restrictions on transporting camels outside Rajasthan. In addition, cultural changes in agricultural practices and security concerns have caused the decline of cattle trade. Due to the availability of modern tractors and other equipment, there is no longer a need for bullocks for agricultural work. Furthermore, farmers have felt security risks in buying cows or buffaloes from the fair, especially due to the fear of attacks on vehicles, as a result of which they prefer local markets. Consequently, not a single buffalo or cow sale was recorded in the latest fair, proving that traditional fairs are now moving away from agricultural needs and becoming display platforms for high-value lifestyle and sports/hobby markets (horse trade), and their protection from regulatory risks is necessary. 19

Section III: Indirect Economic Catalysts: Multiplier Effect, Tourism, and Hospitality

3.1. Micro-Analysis of Tourism Flow: Contribution of Visitors Coming Specifically for the Fair

Traditional fairs have a broad impact on tourism and the hospitality industry, which goes far beyond direct sales. Fairs act as calendar events that motivate national and international tourists to plan a trip to a specific destination. According to the Ministry of Tourism's survey, approximately 39% of visitors were those who had planned their trip specifically to participate in the fair. 0 This ratio clarifies that the calendar of fairs is an important directional factor for national tourism, not just a coincidence of existing travel itineraries. Fairs give a significant boost to tourism and the hospitality industry, leading to a huge increase in demand for registered and unregistered hotels, guest houses, and local transportation services.

3.2. Measurement of Multiplier Effect on Regional Gross Domestic Product (GDP): Assessment of 1.5x to 2x Multiplier Expansion Based on Expenditure

The most powerful economic mechanism of fairs is the multiplier effect. This effect occurs when money spent by any one person in the fair circulates multiple times in the local economy. Economic experts estimate that every rupee spent in the fair stimulates 1.5 to 2 times additional economic activity.

This multiplier effect is highly evident in mega-events. For example, the Maha Kumbh Mela, with its extensive organization and ₹25,000 crore investment, presents a unique scale and generates a massive economic impact. 11 According to economic experts, the total economic impact of this investment and visitor expenditure can range from ₹42,000 crore to ₹112,000 crore. The latest estimates indicate that the total economic activity generated by the Maha Kumbh in 2025 can reach up to ₹2.5 lakh crore. 18 This massive multiplier effect proves that fair-induced consumer spending is a significant enhancer for regional gross domestic product (GDP), creating much more value than short-term sales.

3.3. Broad Pressure and Revenue Benefits on Hospitality and Transportation Sectors

The organization of fairs puts immense pressure on hospitality, transportation, and food and beverage (F&B) industries, which translates into huge revenue benefits. The Haridwar Kumbh Mela provides an excellent example. In this event, the estimated total economic activity was between ₹15,000 crore and ₹20,000 crore. In this total amount, religious tourism and the hospitality sector contributed ₹5,000 crore alone. In addition, revenue of ₹2,500 crore was obtained from transportation and logistics services.

The food and beverage industry is a major beneficiary of this impact. For the Maha Kumbh to be held in Prayagraj, a massive revenue of ₹20,000 crore is estimated for this sector, while the F&B industry saw revenue of about ₹3,500 crore in the Haridwar Kumbh. These figures reflect tremendous economic benefits indirectly for local restaurants, dhabas, catering services, and agricultural producers.

3.4. Tourism 'Spillover' Effect in Small Towns and Remote Areas

An important policy observation is that the economic impact of fairs is not limited to their main event site; it generates a 'spillover' effect in a broad geographical area. Visitors coming from long distances for large events like the Kumbh often plan to extend their trip and visit nearby religious or tourist sites.

For example, due to the Kumbh Fair, significant tourism flow has been seen in surrounding areas like Ayodhya and Varanasi. According to estimates from the Uttar Pradesh Tourism Department, the flow of tourists to these secondary destinations contributes an estimated additional ₹5,000 crore to the regional gross domestic product. This spillover effect economically connects small and remote places that are not part of the direct fair area. This implies that policy planning should not be limited to the fair site but should be developed as part of a broader 'fair circuit,' so that multiple small local markets benefit simultaneously and regional development is balanced.

Section IV: Regional Story of Labor Market and Employment Generation

4.1. Seasonal Employment Opportunities: Increase in Construction, Security, Sanitation, and Administrative Roles

The biggest contribution to the economic role of traditional fairs is seasonal employment generation. Mega-events organize temporary workforce on a large scale, which provides a significant annual boost to local labor markets in areas like construction, logistics, event management, and sanitation.

The example of Kumbh Mela 2025 shows how a massive event creates a temporary labor economy. This event has organized more than 70,000 temporary employees. This workforce includes 15,000 sanitation workers, 45,000 security personnel, and 10,000 administrative staff. 10 These jobs are mainly generated in sectors like construction, logistics, event management, and sanitation, which align with the trends of employment growth in the service and construction sectors at the national level, as reflected in labor force survey data. This temporary workforce not only earns income but also builds local capacity to handle the vast and complex logistics of the fair.

4.2. Impact of Wages and Premium Rate Analysis

Along with seasonal employment, fairs also exert positive pressure on local wage rates during the event. Since the demand for labor peaks during these events, workers often receive higher wage premiums compared to non-festival times. During the Kumbh Mela, an increase of 20 to 30 percent in daily earnings was observed. 17 For example, daily earnings for vendors reached about ₹300 and for boat operators ₹500. This premium reflects an immediate increase in the purchasing power of those workers, who spend a large portion of their income in local markets, further boosting the multiplier effect. This trend provides a seasonal dimension to the overall growth in workforce participation ratio (WPR) seen in both rural and urban areas of India.

4.3. Regional Comparison of Temporary Labor Participation at State/Union Territory Level

In the regional comparison of seasonal labor participation, it was found that the organization of fairs also promotes inter-state labor and cultural exchange. Under the scheme of Zonal Cultural Centres (ZCCs), artists from different states participate in each other's events. This system provides artists with inter-state employment opportunities and establishes an income generation mechanism for performing arts. Thus, the organization of a fair not only benefits local labor but also creates a dynamic market for artists and artisans at the national level.

In addition, a large part of seasonal labor is in the informal sector. Although these workers are not registered under formal government schemes, their increased purchasing power and demand for employment indirectly support the goals of broader government employment generation schemes like the Prime Minister's Employment Generation Programme (PMEGP) and Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Scheme (MGNREGS).

4.4. Role of Employment in the Informal Sector and Question of Social Security

The importance of fairs is not limited to economic investment, but it is also a 'social investment' that brings a community together through temporary employment and strengthens the social fabric. However, even in mega-events that organize more than 70,000 employees, a serious policy challenge emerges: the lack of comprehensive social security and health coverage for temporary and informal workers. Despite high wage premiums, these workers face the instability of seasonal employment. Therefore, it is necessary for the government to integrate specific, limited-duration insurance and social security schemes for the temporary workforce working during these events with existing MSME and rural development programs like PMEGP and SFURTI.

Section V: Long-Term Assets and Sustainable Infrastructure Development

5.1. Permanent Impact of Government Investment: Capital Expenditure on Roads, Flyovers, and Water Supply

The most far-reaching economic impact of traditional fairs is on the sustainable infrastructure development induced by them. Governments view the massive scale of fairs as a necessary incentive for upgrading civic infrastructure, as these events require handling millions of visitors.

The scale of investment for Maha Kumbh 2025 is unprecedented, with the Uttar Pradesh government allocating ₹7,500 crore and the central government ₹2,100 crore, making the total investment more than ₹9,600 crore. 12 This investment is not temporary expenditure but capital expenditure on permanent assets. Analyzing the ₹5,500 crore investment made during the Haridwar Kumbh, this breakdown is clear: ₹1,500 crore was spent on upgrading roads and flyovers, ₹1,000 crore on sanitation and water supply systems (such as river cleaning, waste management), and ₹1,200 crore on temporary housing (tent city). This capital expenditure on roads, water supply, and sanitation systems remains even after the fair, leading to a permanent improvement in the quality of life for local residents and strengthening regional tourism capacity.

5.2. Real Estate and Urban Development: Example of Changes in Property Values After Fairs

The direct impact of infrastructure upgrades induced by fairs is on the local real estate market and urban development. After the Haridwar Kumbh, a significant increase of up to 40% in property values was recorded in the city's major areas, especially near Har Ki Pauri and Jwalapur. This clearly shows that government spending on fairs should be classified as 'capital expenditure' rather than 'revenue expenditure,' as it directly increases regional property values due to better connectivity, sanitation, and civic facilities.

Fairs transform these cities into modern pilgrimage hubs. Government investment promotes the construction of 'smart city' projects and sustainable transportation centers. These developments, especially in hospitality and real estate sectors, create valuable opportunities for sustainable investment, ensuring that the economic benefits of fairs last for generations.

5.3. Analysis of Investment in Supporting Sectors such as Healthcare, Media, and Advertising

The economic impact of fairs extends beyond main trade sectors to supporting sectors like healthcare, media, and advertising, creating a 'third-order effect' in the economy.

Mega-events like Maha Kumbh require advanced healthcare infrastructure to manage crowds of millions. For the Prayagraj Kumbh 2025, revenue of ₹3,000 crore is estimated for the healthcare sector. This not only promotes the construction of temporary health facilities but also strengthens long-term public health preparations. Similarly, the media and advertising sector, which is essential for the coverage and promotion of these events, receives a large economic share. For Maha Kumbh 2025, the estimated revenue from the media and advertising sector is ₹11,000 crore. This data shows that the organization of fairs also affects the development of high-value corporate services and public health infrastructure, leading to the creation of knowledge-based and high-skilled jobs in the local economy.

5.4. Sustainable Legacy Building: Utilization of Civic Infrastructure Left by Fairs

The greatest significance of government investment is the sustainable legacy that fairs leave behind. Expenditure on works like water supply, sewage, waste management, and river cleaning ensures long-term improvements in environmental sustainability and public health for local residents. This ensures that cultural celebrations are aligned with sustainable development goals and strengthen community-led decision-making processes.

Section VI: Challenges of Economic Impact and Evaluation of Efficiency Deficits

6.1. In-Depth Analysis of Economic Leakage: Estimate of Leakage Rate and Its Negative Impact on Local Reinvestment

Despite the economic success of fairs, the rate of economic leakage in India's fairs and tourism systems remains a serious challenge. According to research, economic leakage in Indian tourism systems is estimated to be up to 40%. 29 Leakage refers to the revenue that leaks out of the local economy to external contractors, suppliers, and payments for imported goods.

This high leakage severely reduces reinvestment in local businesses and prevents the multiplier effect from reaching the desired level. Its implication is that if ₹100 is invested, the local community gets a net benefit of only ₹60, while ₹40 goes out. Leakage mainly occurs due to dependence on external investors and specialized production teams, which becomes inevitable in the absence of local capacity building.

6.2. Displacement of Local Vendors and Dominance of External Contractors

Economic leakage is directly related to the displacement of local vendors and the dominance of external contractors. Remote areas or 'hard-to-serve' destinations often lack infrastructure or experienced vendors in terms of expertise required for large-scale events, such as high-level sound engineering, lighting design, stage construction, and professional crowd management.

In such situations, festival organizers prefer to import experts and equipment from established markets to ensure expertise and reliability. This external dependence displaces local vendors and labor. Although developing local talent leads to sustainable capacity building, imported expertise is used to avoid short-term risks, causing benefits to leak out of the local community. If local labor is not trained in event management and technical production, this leakage rate will remain high, hindering the inclusive development goal of fairs.

6.3. Regulatory Barriers and Security Concerns in Trade

The decline of local businesses in some sectors of traditional fairs is not just a result of market forces but a direct consequence of external regulatory and social tensions. The example of Pushkar animal fair clearly highlights this challenge.

The decline of camel and cattle trade in Pushkar is due to a regulatory barrier (ban on transporting camels outside Rajasthan) and social security risks (risk of attacks during transportation). The shrinkage of camel sales to ₹76 lakh and the zeroing of cattle trade show that policy inconsistencies and lack of security can eliminate an entire traditional economic sector. 19 This contradiction becomes more serious when the government runs schemes (SFURTI, ASPIRE) to promote MSMEs and traditional industries on one hand, but on the other hand, some traditional markets do not receive adequate protection from regulatory and security challenges.

6.4. Economic Evaluation of Environmental and Social Costs

Although the economic benefits of fairs are clear, it is necessary to assess the environmental and social costs generated while managing huge crowds. Heavy stress on water resources, high costs of waste management, and the funds required for post-fair restoration of the area deduct from the net economic benefit. In addition, social pressure on local communities increases in the form of temporarily rising rents and resource shortages. The policy framework should focus on minimizing these environmental costs and prioritizing sustainable water and waste management solutions in fair-induced infrastructure, as partially done by investing ₹1,000 crore on river cleaning in the Kumbh.

Section VII: Policy Interventions and Future Strategies

7.1. Government Schemes for MSME Promotion and Their Utilization

To strengthen local businesses, the use of various marketing promotion, skill development, and infrastructure development schemes operated by the government should be done through the platform of fairs. For the upliftment of traditional industries, schemes like Scheme of Fund for Regeneration of Traditional Industries (SFURTI) and Scheme for Promotion of Innovation, Rural Industries and Entrepreneurship (ASPIRE) can provide financial assistance to groups of artisans. The distribution and marketing of these schemes should be done by making fairs an 'outreach hub,' where artisans can get on-site guidance for registration and financial assistance. The Prime Minister's Employment Generation Programme (PMEGP) should give special priority to financing local event management and hospitality MSMEs.

7.2. Digital Platforms and E-Commerce Integration for Traditional Fairs

Digitalization is an important strategy to reduce the instability of seasonal revenue and expand the market reach of artisans. Fairs should be seen not just as sales points but as 'product launchpads.' E-commerce platforms like Epicraft, which are based on MERN technology and use secure payment gateways, provide Indian handicrafts with a global platform.

At the policy level, free digital training and on-boarding facilities should be provided to artisans on the platform in every major fair. This will ensure that even after the sales earned in the fair, artisans can continue to sell their products online for 365 days, ensuring the stability of their income and increasing the profitability of their business.

7.3. Recommendations for Local Capacity Building and Skill Development

To reduce the high rate of 40% economic leakage, it is necessary to train local labor in the specific skills required for large events (such as high-capacity catering, staging, logistics management, and technical event production). This leakage, which occurs due to the import of external experts in event management and technical production, will only decrease when local talent is capable of providing expertise even in 'hard-to-serve' destinations.

Zonal Cultural Centres (ZCCs) need to expand their scope beyond art performances and start 'fair skill development' programs focused on the technical and managerial aspects of event production. These programs should train local youth so that they can replace external experts in sound engineering, lighting design, and crowd management, keeping the benefits of the fair within the local community.

7.4. Specific Financial Models and Incentives for Small Areas in Every State

Financial and regulatory innovation is needed to maximize revenue in local economies. A simple and effective 'micro-tax model' can be implemented to generate revenue from the vast group of unregistered accommodations used by 65% of tourists. This model will impose a minimal fee on unregistered accommodation providers, reducing revenue leakage, and in return, the administration can use this income to provide basic security and sanitation services to visitors.

In addition, to protect traditional businesses, government schemes should link trade security and insurance for livestock breeders (such as Pushkar's livestock breeders). This step will reduce policy risks and security concerns, preventing the decline of traditional markets and allowing them to develop safely in the modern economic framework.

Section VIII: Conclusion and Recommendations of National Importance

8.1. Overall Economic Evaluation of Fairs

The real economic impact of traditional fairs in India is extremely broad and multifaceted, with strategic importance for the national economy. These events are not just seasonal celebrations; they are powerful economic engines that provide direct revenue injection to MSMEs (vendor sales from ₹560 crore to ₹1,400 crore in Kumbh), create massive seasonal employment (more than 70,000 employees in Kumbh), and bring multiplier expansion to regional gross domestic product (GDP) (1.5 to 2 times multiplier effect). This impact is felt in 36 states and union territories.

The most important conclusion is that government investment in fairs (such as ₹5,500 crore on roads, sanitation, and permanent facilities) acts as long-term capital expenditure, increasing regional property values by up to 40%. This clearly establishes fairs as an indispensable policy intervention for strengthening cultural heritage along with building sustainable civic infrastructure.

8.2. Actionable Policy Recommendations for States/Union Territories

Based on in-depth analysis, this report presents the following actionable policy recommendations to ensure maximum benefits for local economies:

  • Leakage Mitigation and Local Capacity Development: To reduce economic leakage up to 40%, each state should start a specific 'Fair Skill Mission' through Zonal Cultural Centers to train local youth in event management and technical production, so that dependence on external contractors can be reduced. 29
  • Formalization of Informal Revenue: A 'micro-tax model' should be implemented to obtain revenue from unregistered accommodations used by 65% of tourists. This will provide the local administration with the necessary revenue for basic facilities.
  • Digital Trade Mandatorization: Under MSME schemes (SFURTI, ASPIRE), on-boarding of artisans on e-commerce platforms (like Epicraft) should be made mandatory, so that their sales can continue for 365 days beyond seasonal events.
  • Fair-Circuit Development: To maximize the 'spillover' effect of mega-events, state governments should develop regional tourism circuits (as seen in the ₹5,000 crore impact for Ayodhya and Varanasi), so that surrounding small areas also receive economic benefits.
  • Traditional Trade Security: To save traditional markets like Pushkar, review state transportation restrictions and make government insurance and security protocols mandatory for livestock sellers to reduce socio-political risks. 19
  • Classification of Capital Expenditure: Fairs' infrastructure investments should be clearly classified as 'capital expenditure,' due to long-term asset creation (real estate value increase).
  • Social Security for Seasonal Labor: Short-term health and accident insurance should be made mandatory for temporary workers (like 70,000 employees) in mega-events, which can be linked to government programs like PMEGP. 10

8.3. Long-Term Vision for Sustainable and Inclusive Development

India's traditional fairs are symbols of the interconnection of economy, culture, and community. To ensure sustainable and inclusive development, fairs should be managed through community-led decision-making processes and effective integration of technology. The potential of these events is to economically strengthen even the smallest areas of India, provided that the policy framework is strengthened to address efficiency deficits (such as 40% leakage) and focus on sustainable capital formation. 29
