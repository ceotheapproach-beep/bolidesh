About Agar Malwa District

Agar Malwa district, located in the Malwa region of Madhya Pradesh, India, is known for its historical significance as part of the Scindia state, agricultural economy focusing on soybeans and wheat, and religious sites like temples. It features plateaus, rivers like Chhoti Kali Sindh, and a blend of cultural heritage with modern developments.

History

Agar Malwa district was a division during the Sindhia state, with some of their palaces still presently used for city court and for other government offices. It was formerly a cantonment region at the time of India's independence because of the favourable weather and the availability of water. It was a district under the state of Madhya Bharat after the independence of India until 1956. Since 16 August 2013, Agar Malwa has been the 51st district of Madhya Pradesh. The district was formed by removing Agar, Badod, Susner and Nalkheda tehsils from Shajapur District, decreasing its size. The region has roots in medieval times, with influences from Rajput, Maratha, and British eras. Historical landmarks include Scindia-era structures and ancient temples dating back centuries. The area was part of the Malwa plateau's political landscape, witnessing battles and alliances. Post-independence, it integrated into Madhya Bharat, reflecting administrative reorganizations. Cultural history includes folk traditions, festivals, and artisan crafts preserved over generations. Geography

The western part of the district is marked by the Agar Plateau that covers the major areas of Agar Malwa district. There is a hill tract to the west of the town of Badod, showing scattered hillocks in a north–south direction. The presence of hills in the center has affected the drainage pattern. The height of this tract varies between 500 metres (1,600 ft) and 545 metres (1,788 ft) above the mean sea level and it slopes towards the north. Dudhaliya and Kachhol are the main streams in the west, originating from the hill tract and draining towards the west. Chhoti Kali Sindh, which is the main perennial stream of the region, flows northwards on the western border of the region. The district's coordinates are 23°42′54″N 76°00′47″E. The total area is 2,785 km², part of the Malwa Plateau with fertile black soil ideal for agriculture. It borders Rajgarh, Shajapur, Ujjain, and Ratlam districts. Climate is tropical with hot summers and moderate rainfall. Geological features include basalt rocks and river valleys supporting biodiversity. Statistics and Demographics

According to the 2011 census, Agar Malwa district had a population of 571,278, of which 117,329 (20.54%) lived in urban areas. Agar Malwa had a sex ratio of 949 females per 1000 males. Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes make up 135,898 (23.79%) and 13,941 (2.44%) of the population respectively. The literacy rate is 51%. The population density is 205.1/km² (531.3/sq mi). Religions: Hinduism 90.83%, Islam 7.58%, Jainism 1.48%, Other 0.11%. Languages: Malvi 53.24%, Hindi 41.31%, Sondwari 4.80%, Others 0.58%. No full 2021 census data available, but estimates suggest population around 5.71 lakhs. Administrative: 2 sub-divisions, 4-5 tehsils, 227 gram panchayats, 512-513 villages. Economy agriculture-based: soybeans, wheat, gram. Tourist Spots

Baijnath Mahadev Temple: Ancient Shiva temple in Agar town, known for architecture and festivals. Baglamukhi Mata Temple: In Nalkheda on Lakhundar River banks, one of eight Shakti Peethas, attracts devotees for tantric rituals. Mothisagar Pond (Big Pond): Scenic artificial lake in Agar, ideal for picnics and boating. Someshwar Mahadev Temple: Historic Shiva shrine with intricate carvings. Maa Tulja Bhavani: Revered temple dedicated to goddess. Pachetti Mata Temple: Local deity worship site in rural setting. Chausath Yogini Temple: Ancient temple complex with 64 yogini statues, historical significance. Lal Burj: Red tower near Kewai River, offering views. Kewai River: Tranquil spots for nature walks and picnics. Other minor spots: Motisagar Talab, various small temples like Ganesh Mandir, local forts ruins, agricultural fields viewpoints, and cultural heritage sites scattered in tehsils.
