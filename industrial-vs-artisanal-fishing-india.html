Industrial vs. Artisanal Fishing in India: Ecological, Economic, and Social Conflicts

September 28, 2025 Culture

Contents

  • Background
  • Economic and Social Costs
  • Policy Framework and Regional Conflicts
  • Solutions

Background

India’s coastal fisheries face a deepening conflict between industrial and artisanal fishing, threatening ecosystems and traditional livelihoods. With a marine fish yield of ~5.31 million tons annually, pressure concentrates within 12 nautical miles, the domain of artisanal fishers. The Bolidesh Foundation highlights how this clash endangers cultural heritage and ecological balance.

Fleet Structure: Artisanal fishers (~2 lakh boats, ≤10 HP) use sustainable methods, while industrial fleets (~75,000 trawlers, ≥150 HP) overexploit juvenile fish and damage seabeds. Foreign vessels in India’s Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) add indirect pressure.

Economic and Social Costs

Environmental Damage: Industrial trawling destroys fish nurseries and benthic fauna, reducing catches for artisanal fishers. Fuel subsidies for industrial fleets lower fish prices, making competition impossible for small-scale fishers.

Rights Violations: Mechanized boats encroach on the 12-nautical-mile artisanal zone, violating the Marine Fishing Regulation Act (MFRA). Conservation measures, like Odisha’s 7-month Olive Ridley turtle fishing ban, leave 97% of fishers uncompensated, pushing them toward poverty.

Social Disruption: Livelihood losses drive youth migration, eroding traditional knowledge and community structures, often ignored by media focused on industrial GDP contributions.

Policy Framework and Regional Conflicts

Policy Gaps: The Blue Revolution and PMMSY prioritize modernization, sidelining artisanal fishers who lack capital for advanced technologies. Weak regulation of foreign fleets exacerbates resource depletion.

Regional Conflicts: In Kerala, trawling ban violations spark protests led by groups like the National Fishermen Forum. In Odisha and Andhra Pradesh, turtle conservation bans disrupt livelihoods without adequate compensation.

Media Bias: Industrial fishing’s economic benefits overshadow artisanal struggles, with complex ecological and social costs rarely covered due to industrial lobbying.

Solutions

To resolve this conflict, India should:

  • Enforce Mesh Size Regulations: Use CMFRI data to regulate juvenile fishing and penalize non-compliant industrial fleets.
  • Internalize Environmental Costs: Impose fines on industrial fleets for seabed damage and bycatch to reflect true ecological costs.
  • Promote Co-Management: Establish Traditional Fisheries Management Areas (TFMAs) to empower artisanal fishers in resource governance.
  • Enhance Compensation: Provide timely compensation and alternative livelihoods, especially for women, during fishing bans.

The Bolidesh Foundation advocates for equitable policies to protect artisanal fishers’ rights and coastal heritage. Contact us to support this mission.
