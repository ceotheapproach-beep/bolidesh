Displacement of Coastal Fishing Communities in India: Industrial Expansion, Regulatory Failure, and Social Disruption

Executive Summary

India’s coastal fishing communities face a crisis driven by industrial port expansion, land reclamation, and regulatory failures, leading to displacement, livelihood loss, and social disruption. Weak enforcement of Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) norms and inadequate rehabilitation fuel economic instability and youth migration. The Vizhinjam port conflict in Kerala exemplifies these issues, with coastal erosion and insufficient compensation sparking protests. Solutions include stricter CRZ enforcement, land-for-land rehabilitation, and inclusive ‘blue skilling’ programs. The Bolidesh Foundation advocates for sustainable, equitable development to protect these communities.

Introduction

India’s coastal fishing communities, integral to the $8 billion marine export industry, face existential threats from industrial expansion and regulatory lapses. Supporting over 28 million livelihoods, these communities embody a symbiotic relationship with coastal ecosystems, rooted in traditional knowledge. However, port development, mangrove loss, and CRZ violations erode their livelihoods, driving social and cultural disruption. This report examines these challenges and proposes solutions to align the Blue Economy with social and ecological justice, in line with Bolidesh Foundation’s mission.

Industrial Interventions

Port and Jetty Expansion: Large-scale dredging and land reclamation for ports like Vizhinjam disrupt fishing access and cause coastal erosion, displacing entire villages. In Mumbai, coastal infrastructure like the Bandra-Worli Sea Link reduced non-mechanized boats’ daily catch by 50% (from 14 kg to 7 kg).

Mangrove Degradation: Mangroves, vital for fish nurseries, have declined significantly due to industrial encroachment and pollution, threatening fish stocks and community livelihoods.

Regulatory Failures

The CRZ Notification (2011) mandates prior clearance for coastal projects, but a 2021 MoEFCC memo allows violators to regularize projects with fines, undermining enforcement. This ‘pay-and-permit’ system favors industrial interests, as seen in Tamil Nadu’s Karikattukuppam Beach case. Inadequate rehabilitation, like Vizhinjam’s ₹10 lakh offer, fails to address communities’ needs, especially for those lacking formal land titles.

Social Disruption

Declining fish catches force fishers, especially women, into low-wage informal jobs, exacerbating gender disparities. Youth migration to urban centers, driven by livelihood loss, erodes traditional knowledge and community leadership. Violent suppression of protests, as in Vizhinjam, criminalizes dissent, further marginalizing communities.

Case Study: Vizhinjam Conflict

The Vizhinjam port project in Kerala triggered coastal erosion and village displacement due to extensive dredging and seawall construction. Communities rejected the ₹10 lakh compensation, demanding land-for-land rehabilitation. Protests faced brutal police response in 2022, with over 1,000 charged, highlighting state bias toward industrial interests and weak environmental impact assessments.

Data Gaps and Empowerment

The Marine Fisheries Census (MFC) 2025, using the VyAS-NAV app, will collect digital, geotagged data on migration and socio-economic conditions, aiding evidence-based governance. Initiatives like artificial reefs in Mumbai and mangrove restoration by CMFRI mitigate damage but fall short without addressing root causes like CRZ violations.

Policy Recommendations

To empower coastal communities, India should:

  • Strengthen CRZ Enforcement: Repeal the 2021 ‘pay-and-permit’ memo and enforce NGT orders to protect coastal ecosystems.
  • Adopt Land-for-Land Rehabilitation: Prioritize land-based compensation over monetary offers to restore community identity.
  • Formalize Land Titles: Provide legal support for communities lacking land ownership records to secure compensation.
  • Promote Blue Skilling: Launch programs integrating traditional knowledge with sustainable practices like mariculture and eco-tourism, targeting youth and women.
  • Enhance Gender Inclusion: Prioritize women’s employment in Blue Economy policies, recognizing their role as stakeholders.
  • Leverage MFC 2025 Data: Use census data to address migration and develop targeted welfare schemes.

Conclusion

India’s coastal fishing communities face displacement and social disruption from industrial expansion and regulatory failures. The Blue Economy’s focus on production overlooks ecological and social costs, as seen in Vizhinjam. By strengthening CRZ enforcement, adopting equitable rehabilitation, and empowering communities through skilling, India can balance development with justice.
