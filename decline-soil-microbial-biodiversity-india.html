Decline of Soil Microbial Biodiversity in India: Ecological and Socio-Economic Impacts of Intensive Agriculture

September 28, 2025 Culture

Introduction

Soil is often seen as a mere foundation for agriculture, but it is one of the planet’s most complex and vibrant ecosystems. At its core lies microbial biodiversity—bacteria, fungi, actinomycetes, and protozoa—that sustains soil health and fertility. In India, where agriculture supports millions of livelihoods and smallholder farmers manage nearly 80% of farmland, soil health directly impacts food security and economic stability. This article explores the decline of soil microbial biodiversity due to intensive agricultural practices, its ecological and socio-economic consequences, and sustainable solutions rooted in traditional knowledge and modern science, aligning with the Bolidesh Foundation’s mission to preserve cultural heritage.

Importance of Soil Microbes

Soil microbes perform critical functions, including nutrient cycling (e.g., nitrogen fixation by Rhizobium, phosphorus solubilization), carbon sequestration, and soil structure maintenance. A diverse microbiome enhances water retention, suppresses pathogens, and boosts plant resilience. In India’s diverse agro-climatic zones, these microbial services are vital for sustaining traditional farming practices, which have historically relied on organic inputs and crop diversity to maintain soil vitality.

Drivers of Decline: Intensive Agriculture

Since the Green Revolution, India’s agriculture has shifted toward intensive systems reliant on chemical fertilizers (NPK), monoculture, and crop residue mismanagement. These practices have led to a rapid decline in microbial biodiversity, with severe ecological and socio-economic consequences:

  • Chemical Fertilizers: Overuse of NPK fertilizers increases soil salinity and pH imbalances, suppressing beneficial microbes like Rhizobium and mycorrhizal fungi.
  • Monoculture: Single-crop systems reduce microbial diversity by limiting root exudates, leading to pathogen accumulation and soil fatigue.
  • Crop Residue Burning: Burning residues depletes soil organic matter (SOM), destroying microbial habitats and reducing nutrient availability.

These practices have eroded traditional, sustainable farming knowledge, replacing it with chemical-dependent systems that undermine long-term soil health.

Ecological Analysis

The rhizosphere, the soil zone around plant roots, is a hotspot for microbial activity. Intensive agriculture disrupts this zone, impairing nutrient cycling and carbon storage. Chemical fertilizers create osmotic stress, killing sensitive microbes, while pesticides and herbicides have non-target effects, reducing beneficial fungi and bacteria. This loss weakens soil structure, reduces water retention, and increases vulnerability to erosion and climate stressors, threatening India’s agricultural resilience.

Socio-Economic and Cultural Impacts

The decline in microbial biodiversity has profound socio-economic impacts, particularly for smallholder farmers. Increased reliance on chemical inputs raises costs, deepening financial burdens and debt. Moreover, the erosion of traditional knowledge—such as mixed cropping and organic manure use—threatens cultural heritage. This loss disrupts the intergenerational transfer of sustainable practices, undermining community identity and resilience. The Bolidesh Foundation’s local initiatives aim to revive these practices to support both ecology and culture.

Policy Responses and Evaluation

India’s Soil Health Card (SHC) scheme promotes balanced fertilizer use, reducing urea consumption by 9% and increasing yields by 5–6%. However, its focus on chemical nutrient management overlooks microbial health. The Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana (PKVY) supports organic farming, encouraging biofertilizers and sustainable practices, which align with microbial restoration. Integrated Pest Management (IPM) further reduces chemical pesticide use, protecting non-target microbes. While these policies show promise, they need to integrate microbial profiling for holistic soil health management.

Sustainable Solutions and Roadmap

To restore soil microbial biodiversity, India must adopt an agro-ecological approach combining traditional knowledge and modern science:

  • Mixed Cropping and Crop Rotation: These practices diversify root exudates, enhancing microbial diversity and reducing pathogen buildup.
  • Organic and Biofertilizers: Compost and biofertilizers like Azotobacter and Rhizobium replenish soil organic matter and microbial populations.
  • Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Using bio-pesticides minimizes harm to beneficial microbes.
  • Microbiome Profiling in Precision Agriculture: Incorporate microbial data into soil testing to assess biological functionality, not just chemical content.
  • National Soil Health Mission: Upgrade the SHC scheme to include microbial indicators, leveraging existing infrastructure for scalable impact.
  • Traditional Knowledge Integration: Document and promote regional farming practices, offering incentives for sustainable methods.

Conclusion

The decline of soil microbial biodiversity poses a critical threat to India’s food security, ecological stability, and cultural heritage. Intensive agriculture has degraded soil health, increased costs for farmers, and eroded traditional knowledge. By integrating microbial-focused policies, agro-ecological practices, and cultural preservation, India can restore its soils and ensure sustainable agriculture. The Bolidesh Foundation advocates for these solutions, supporting communities in preserving their heritage while building resilient ecosystems.
