Climate activist Sonam Wangchuk arrested in Leh: Ladakh movement and national security clash (26 September 2025)

26 September 2025 Leh, Ladakh National / Politics

26 September 2025 proved a decisive day for Ladakh — the strategically sensitive Union Territory witnessed a major political upheaval when prominent educator, inventor and climate activist Sonam Wangchuk was arrested by Leh Police. The detention, carried out under the stringent National Security Act (NSA), followed violent protests over demands for full statehood for Ladakh and the region's inclusion under the Constitution's Sixth Schedule. The unrest has sharpened a national debate over freedom of expression, the right to protest in a democracy, and the Centre's administrative approach to local dissent.

Background: The 'Sixth Schedule' demand and the movement for statehood

The arrest is rooted in a five-year mobilisation calling for statehood for Ladakh and for constitutional protections under the Sixth Schedule — measures activists say are needed to secure local land rights, resources and cultural identity following Ladakh's reconstitution as a Union Territory in 2019. Protesters have also demanded guaranteed local employment and reservation for Ladakhi youth in government jobs. Wangchuk, internationally known for innovations such as the ice stupa and for founding the educational initiative SECMOL, emerged as a prominent face of the movement and led hunger strikes and marches that intensified public attention.

Violence of 24 September and immediate aftermath

On 24 September, demonstrations in Leh escalated into violent clashes between protesters — including many young people — and security forces. Reports say protesters attempted to set fire to political offices and security vehicles; police used batons, tear gas and, according to multiple accounts, opened fire during the confrontation. The clashes resulted in the deaths of four protesters and left dozens injured; authorities imposed a comprehensive curfew and deployed large numbers of CRPF and ITBP personnel to restore order. Mobile internet was also suspended in Leh as a security measure.

Arrest and official allegations

Two days after the violence, on 26 September, Leh Police — led by the DGP's office — arrested Sonam Wangchuk from his village. The Union Home Ministry and local authorities accused him of making "provocative statements" and of inciting young protesters, asserting that his speeches and hunger strike contributed to the unrest. Wangchuk has denied these allegations and says he repeatedly called for non-violence; the government, however, has invoked the NSA to justify his detention and moved him out of Ladakh to a central prison facility.

Action against SECMOL and financial allegations

A day before Wangchuk's arrest, the Union Home Ministry cancelled the FCRA (Foreign Contribution Regulation Act) licence of SECMOL, the school and NGO Wangchuk founded, citing alleged financial irregularities and compliance violations. Authorities say investigations (including CBI inquiries reported in the press) have been opened into these matters; Wangchuk's supporters claim the move was an administrative retaliation aimed at weakening the movement.

Supporters' rebuttal and family statements

Wangchuk and his supporters have rejected all charges. He maintains his commitment to peaceful protest and says he appealed to people to remain calm after violence broke out. Family members and colleagues have condemned the arrest as heavy-handed; his wife publicly accused authorities of treating him like a criminal without due process and alleged forceful entry and damage to their home. Supporters describe the FCRA cancellation as punitive.

National reaction and political fallout

The arrest quickly reverberated across national politics. Opposition parties described the detention as authoritarian and voiced concerns about the shrinking space for peaceful dissent. Leaders from regional parties and civil society raised alarms over the use of preventive security laws against a prominent public figure. Social media became polarized with competing hashtags and campaigns both supporting and condemning Wangchuk. Human rights organisations and several news outlets have highlighted the gravity of the events and called for scrutiny of the use of force and of preventive detention powers.

What next: legal and political implications

Wangchuk's legal team has signalled its intention to challenge the NSA detention and the FCRA cancellation in courts. The arrest has removed a leading face from the movement's public leadership but — according to observers cited in media reports — the action risks further inflaming popular sentiment in Ladakh and complicating any political settlement. For the central government, the episode underscores the difficult balance between maintaining public order in a strategically sensitive region and responding to long-standing local grievances over identity, land and jobs.
