Genetic Erosion of Traditional Seeds: A Critical Threat to India’s Agricultural Resilience, Food Security, and Climate Adaptation

Introduction

Genetic erosion—the loss of genetic diversity in crop species and varieties—threatens India’s agricultural resilience, food security, and climate adaptation. Traditional seeds, or landraces, adapted to local conditions, are being replaced by high-yielding varieties (HYVs) due to policy biases and market forces. This erosion weakens crops’ ability to withstand pests, diseases, and climate variability, endangering livelihoods and cultural heritage. The Bolidesh Foundation advocates for preserving these seeds to sustain India’s agro-ecological and cultural legacy.

Legacy of the Green Revolution

The Green Revolution boosted India’s food production, achieving 131 million tons of grain by 1978–79, primarily through HYVs like wheat and rice. While HYVs increased yields (e.g., hybrid tomatoes at 30–40 tons/acre vs. traditional varieties at 15–25 tons/acre), their reliance on intensive irrigation, fertilizers, and monoculture marginalized traditional seeds. Policies like the Public Distribution System (PDS) prioritized HYVs, sidelining nutritious, climate-resilient crops like millets and pulses, laying the foundation for genetic erosion.

Policy and Market Drivers

India’s agricultural policies have favored HYVs through procurement, minimum support prices (MSP), and subsidies for fertilizers and irrigation, neglecting traditional crops. Research and development (R&D) focus on commercial crops, reducing biodiversity. Corporate seed companies dominate with non-reusable F1 hybrid seeds, eroding farmers’ seed sovereignty and creating dependency on costly inputs. Reports of corruption, including ₹50 lakh in fake seed scandals, further undermine trust in seed systems, exacerbating economic risks for smallholder farmers.

Long-Term Impacts

Genetic uniformity increases vulnerability to pests, diseases, and extreme weather, risking crop failures. HYVs’ high water and input demands strain resources, unlike resilient traditional seeds suited for rainfed areas (50% of India’s farmland). The shift to polished HYV grains has reduced dietary diversity, contributing to rising lifestyle diseases like diabetes. Economically, dependency on costly inputs raises production costs, driving debt and rural-to-urban migration, threatening ecological and economic stability.

Conservation Frameworks

India’s National Bureau of Plant Genetic Resources (NBPGR) manages the National Gene Bank, preserving over 468,000 accessions, including 340,515 indigenous varieties. The Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers’ Rights (PPV&FR) Act, 2001, safeguards farmers’ rights to save, reuse, and exchange seeds, countering corporate control. The Biodiversity Act, 2002, ensures equitable benefit-sharing. However, effective utilization of preserved germplasm remains limited, requiring stronger on-farm (in-situ) integration.

Community Seed Banks

Community Seed Banks (CSBs) empower farmers by preserving traditional seeds and promoting seed independence. In Odisha’s Raysar village, a CSB expanded from 12 to 52 rice varieties, boosting economic returns (from ₹1,200 to ₹2,000/quintal) through organic certification and premium pricing. These banks enhance climate resilience, soil health, and women’s participation, preserving cultural knowledge. Initiatives like Uttarakhand’s Beej Bachao Andolan and Maharashtra’s Rahibai Popere’s efforts further demonstrate successful in-situ conservation.

Policy Recommendations

To reverse genetic erosion, India must adopt an agro-ecological approach:

  • Policy Shift: Redirect subsidies and R&D to support traditional varieties, especially in rainfed areas.
  • Market Integration: Promote premium pricing for traditional crops through schemes like NMSA and FPOs, ensuring economic viability.
  • Strengthen PPV&FR Act: Enhance enforcement to protect farmers’ rights and curb corporate malpractices like fake seeds.
  • Germplasm Repatriation: Bridge ex-situ and in-situ conservation by integrating NBPGR’s germplasm into CSBs.
  • Knowledge Preservation: Document indigenous seed practices in educational curricula and community archives.
  • Integrated Systems: Promote diversified farming systems combining crops, livestock, and agroforestry for resilience.

Conclusion

Genetic erosion of traditional seeds threatens India’s agricultural resilience, food security, and cultural heritage. While NBPGR and PPV&FR provide robust frameworks, effective utilization and market integration are critical. Community Seed Banks, like Odisha’s Raysar model, show that value addition can make traditional varieties economically viable. By aligning policies with agro-ecology and cultural preservation, India can ensure sustainable agriculture. The Bolidesh Foundation’s initiatives support these efforts.
