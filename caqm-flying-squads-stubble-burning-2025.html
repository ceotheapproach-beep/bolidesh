Delhi's Battle Against Toxic Air: CAQM Deploys Special 'Flying Squads' to Punjab-Haryana Stubble Hotspots (27 September 2025)

27 September 2025 New Delhi/Chandigarh, India National / Environment

As winter approaches in North India, the looming threat of air pollution prompts aggressive action. The Commission for Air Quality Management (CAQM) has deployed special 'flying squads' to hotspot districts in Punjab and Haryana to prevent stubble burning, a primary contributor to Delhi-NCR's severe winter smog. 0 1 2 This zero-tolerance initiative, supported by a new coordination cell in Mohali, emphasizes real-time monitoring and enforcement to safeguard public health. 1 5

The Stubble Burning Crisis: Fueling Delhi's Smog

Stubble burning peaks in October-November as Punjab and Haryana farmers quickly clear paddy residues for wheat sowing, releasing toxic smoke that travels to Delhi via slow winds, trapping PM2.5 and PM10 particles in low temperatures. 3 This annual ritual exacerbates respiratory diseases, heart conditions, and child health issues, imposing a heavy economic burden on healthcare systems. 0

Mission and Model of the 'Flying Squads'

These squads, empowered with quasi-judicial authority, go beyond monitoring to enforce compliance rapidly. 2

1. Hotspot Focus: Deployed in 10-12 key districts identified over three years, including Punjab's Sangrur, Patiala, Bathinda, and Haryana's Kaithal, Karnal. 6 8

2. Tech-Driven Surveillance: Utilizing ISRO/NASA satellite fire imagery for real-time detection, GPS vehicles, and drones to reach sites swiftly. 7

3. Strict Enforcement and Penalties: Immediate fines of ₹2,500–₹15,000 under environmental compensation, with FIRs for repeat offenders. 3 5

Table 1: Key Hotspot Districts and Squad Deployment
State Districts Deployment Focus
Punjab Sangrur, Patiala, Bathinda, Amritsar, Ludhiana High stubble incidents; real-time satellite monitoring 6
Haryana Kaithal, Karnal, Ambala, Hisar, Sirsa Crop residue hotspots; drone patrols 8

Administrative and Political Challenges

Success hinges on cooperation from Punjab and Haryana administrations, amid farmer protests labeling bans 'anti-farmer'. 3 CAQM mandates subsidized machinery like Happy Seeder and alternative uses for stubble, with squads overseeing compliance. 1 9 Farmers demand incentives over penalties due to high machinery costs. 0

Global Implications: A Universal Air Pollution Challenge

Stubble burning mirrors issues in China and Indonesia, with WHO warning Delhi's pollution shortens life expectancy. 3 If successful, this model could inspire enforcement in agriculture-heavy regions, aligning with India's climate commitments by curbing greenhouse gas emissions. 2

Conclusion

CAQM's flying squads symbolize resolve and proactive enforcement, shifting from advisories to direct action. 0 Their effectiveness amid political and social hurdles will determine Delhi's air quality and the health of millions in North India. 9
