About Anjaw District

Anjaw district, located in the easternmost part of Arunachal Pradesh, India, is known for its strategic border location with China and Myanmar, rich biodiversity, and tribal cultures including the Mishmi and Meyor tribes. It features turbulent rivers, snow-capped mountains, and vast forests, making it a paradise of natural beauty and cultural heritage.

History

Anjaw District was bifurcated from Lohit District on 16th February 2004 with its Headquarter at Hawai at an altitude of 1296m above sea level. The District was established with seven Administrative circles Namely Hayuliang, Goiliang, Manchal, Chaglagam, Walong, Hawai and Kibithoo but at present has eight circles with Metengliang being the latest circle to be notified. The administrative history traces back to British India, where prior to 1914, the area was part of Lakhimpur district. In 1914, the North East Frontier Tract was created, and the area fell under the Central and Eastern Section. In 1919, it became part of Sadiya Frontier Tract. In 1943, Tirap Frontier Tract was formed. In 1948, under the North-East Frontier Tract Regulation, the Mishmi Hills District was created for Mishmi-inhabited areas. In 1952, headquarters shifted to Tezu, and Hayuliang was upgraded. In 1954, it became Lohit Frontier Division. In 1965, Lohit district was formed. In 1980, Dibang Valley was bifurcated. Anjaw was carved out in 2004. During the 1962 Sino-Indian War, parts were occupied by China, and military presence remains due to border disputes. In 2020 skirmishes, additional troops were deployed. The region has been home to Mishmi tribes, preserving animist beliefs and unique customs. The Mishmi Hills District highlighted the cultural significance of indigenous communities. Historical events include British administrative reorganizations to manage frontier areas, and post-independence integrations into NEFA (North-East Frontier Agency). The district's formation aimed to improve governance in remote border areas. Cultural history involves spiritual beliefs in supreme beings like Jabmalu and Ringya, traditional arbitration systems like Kabeya and Pharai, and festivals. Geography

Located at the easternmost part of Arunachal Pradesh, the district lies between 27°33' – 29°22' North Latitude and 95°15' to 97°24' East Longitude. Spread over 6190 sq km, it is home to numerous turbulent streams, roaring rivers like the Lohit (tributary of Brahmaputra), snow-clad mountains, and thousands of rare species of flora and fauna. Bounded by China to the north, Myanmar to the east, Lower Dibang Valley and Lohit to the west, and Changlang to the south. It features lush hills, deep valleys of the Himalayas, virgin forests, foot suspension bridges, and pleasant winds. The drive from Tezu to Kibithoo via Tiding, Hayuliang, Hawai, and Walong is fascinating along the Lohit River. Rich in wildlife: Mishmi takin, red goral, Gongshan muntjac, leaf muntjac, Sclater's monal, Pinus merkusii pine, and Mishmi Hills Giant Flying Squirrel. Recently, white-cheeked macaque noted. Proposed Mago-Thingbu to Vijaynagar Frontier Highway along McMahon Line. Land of hundreds of waterfalls, enchanting beauty with diverse topography from tropical forests to alpine meadows. Rivers carve deep gorges, supporting biodiversity hotspots. Climate varies from subtropical to alpine, with heavy rainfall. Geological features include Himalayan foothills, seismic activity due to tectonics. Vegetation zones: tropical evergreen, subtropical broadleaf, temperate coniferous. Fauna includes endangered species protected in sanctuaries. Infrastructure challenges due to terrain, but strategic roads enhance connectivity. Statistics and Demographics

According to 2011 census, population 21,167 (11,507 male, 9,660 female), 3rd lowest density in India at 3.42/km². Growth rate 13.77% (2001-2011). Sex ratio 805/1000. Literacy 59.4%. ST population 16,451 (77.72%): Idu/Taraon Mishmi (9,991), Kaman/Miju Mishmi (5,021), Digaru Mishmi (472), Meyor/Zakhring (472). Languages: Mishmi (72.3%), Hindi (7.03%), Nepali (5.27%), etc. Religion: Hinduism (61.83%), Donyi Polo (30.75%), Islam (3.09%), Buddhism (3.04%), Christianity (1.28%). Workforce: Main workers 9,264 (5,715 male, 3,549 female), Marginal 1,237, Non-workers 10,666. Tribes: Tawrah/Digaru Mishmi in Hayuliang/Chaglagam, Kaman/Miju in Goiliang/Manchal/Hawai, Meyor in Kibithoo/Walong. Economy agriculture-dependent: maize, millet, rice, beans, cardamom, fruits. Low population supports conservation. Recent data: PMMVY enrollment 354% achievement (2025). Tourist Spots

Kaho: Last border village with China, inhabited by Meyor, features Gonpa built in 1960. Kibithoo: Remotest circle HQ, on Lohit right bank, 1305m altitude, visible border, surrounded by streams. Namti Valley: 7km from Walong, known as Namti Maidan, scenic plains. Dong: Picturesque Meyor village 7km from Walong. Hot Spring (Tilam): Along Lohit in low-lying area. Helmet Top: Above Namti, memorial for soldiers. Walong: 1094m altitude, 58km from Hawai, historical significance from 1962 war, War Memorial. Old Hawai: Twin villages Walla/Watong 3km from Hawai. Hawai: District HQ on Lohit left bank, Kaman Mishmi lore. Chaglagam: On Dalai River left bank, 1258m, highest large cardamom yield. Other small spots: Karang Tuwi Lake (5km crystal clear freshwater near Chaglagam), Hayuliang township, Metengliang circle.
