Erosion of Plant and Animal Genetic Diversity in India: Crisis for Agricultural System Resilience and Policy Responses

September 28, 2025 Culture

Introduction

Agro-biodiversity, encompassing ecosystems, species, and genetic diversity, is vital for sustainable agriculture. In India, genetic erosion—the rapid loss of indigenous plant and animal varieties—threatens agricultural resilience, food security, and climate adaptation. Driven by intensive farming, hybrid varieties, and cross-breeding, this erosion undermines the ability of crops and livestock to adapt to environmental shocks. The Bolidesh Foundation champions the preservation of these genetic resources as part of India’s cultural heritage.

Drivers of Genetic Erosion

Livestock: Economic pressures push farmers toward cross-breeding high-yielding exotic breeds, as indigenous cattle produce only 73 kg of milk annually compared to 3,200–3,700 kg for foreign breeds. This shift erodes native breeds like Jafarabadi and Mehsani buffaloes, adapted to local conditions. The National Bureau of Animal Genetic Resources (NBAGR) has registered eight new breeds, but displacement continues.

Plants: Monoculture and genetically modified (GM) crops prioritize yield over diversity, increasing risks from pests and diseases. GM crops may reduce pesticide use but harm beneficial species and raise concerns about antibiotic-resistant genes. The National Bureau of Plant Genetic Resources (NBPGR) preserves over 468,000 seed accessions, yet on-farm diversity declines.

Impact on Resilience

Genetic erosion weakens agricultural systems’ ability to withstand climate shocks. Indigenous livestock, with superior heat tolerance (e.g., higher antioxidant capacity, lower HSP70 expression), outperform cross-breeds in tropical conditions. Their loss increases vulnerability to heat stress. Similarly, traditional crop varieties, adapted to local soils and climates, offer natural resistance to pests and droughts, unlike uniform hybrids. This loss forces reliance on costly chemical inputs, degrading soil and water resources.

Regional Analysis and Community Innovation

Genetic erosion erases local knowledge, such as the pineapple-based agroforestry system (PAFS) in Northeast India’s Hmar community, a sustainable alternative to shifting cultivation. In Gujarat and Rajasthan, displacement of drought-tolerant buffalo breeds disrupts pastoral systems. Collective farming and Farmer Producer Organizations (FPOs) in Jharkhand empower small farmers to maintain diverse crops, enhancing economic and nutritional security.

Legal and Institutional Frameworks

India’s robust frameworks include:

  • PPV&FR Act, 2001: Protects farmers’ rights to save, reuse, and exchange seeds, ensuring benefit-sharing.
  • Biodiversity Act, 2002: Promotes sustainable use and equitable benefit-sharing of biological resources.
  • NBAGR and NBPGR: Preserve livestock and plant genetic resources, with NBPGR’s National Gene Bank storing 468,000+ accessions.

Challenges include limited farmer awareness and enforcement gaps, risking resource misuse. The Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL) prevents biopiracy, addressing ~2,000 annual patent misuse cases.

Policy Recommendations

To reverse genetic erosion, India should:

  • Prioritize In-Situ Conservation: Support farmer-led programs with financial incentives to maintain traditional varieties on-farm.
  • Resilience-Based Breeding: Shift R&D to focus on heat tolerance, disease resistance, and drought adaptation, leveraging indigenous traits.
  • Strengthen Market Linkages: Promote collective farming and FPOs to create value chains for traditional products, ensuring economic viability.
  • Enhance Legal Enforcement: Improve awareness and enforcement of PPV&FR Act to protect farmers’ rights and curb biopiracy.
  • Integrate Disaster Planning: Prioritize drought-tolerant breeds in disaster management plans, securing fodder reserves.

Conclusion

The erosion of plant and animal genetic diversity threatens India’s agricultural resilience and cultural heritage. While institutions like NBAGR and NBPGR, alongside laws like PPV&FR and Biodiversity Acts, provide a strong foundation, effective implementation is critical. By prioritizing in-situ conservation, resilience-based breeding, and market integration, India can safeguard its agro-biodiversity. The Bolidesh Foundation’s initiatives support these efforts.
