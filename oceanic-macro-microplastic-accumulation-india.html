Oceanic Macro- and Microplastic Accumulation in India: Pathways, Food Chain Integration, and Long-Term Health Risks

Introduction

India’s 7,500-km coastline faces a growing crisis from macro- and microplastic pollution, threatening ecosystems, food security, and public health. With 51 trillion microplastic particles globally, India’s high population density and poor waste management amplify the issue. This report analyzes plastic waste pathways, regional impacts, and health risks, aligning with the Bolidesh Foundation’s mission to protect environmental and cultural heritage.

Plastic Waste Pathways

Land-Based Sources: About 80% of ocean plastic originates from land, with India among the top global polluters due to mismanaged waste. In Prayagraj, ~8 tons of plastic leak daily into rivers. Poor enforcement of Solid Waste Management Rules (2016) exacerbates this.

Riverine Transfer: Rivers like the Ganga and Brahmaputra, among the world’s most plastic-polluted, transport 15-20% of ocean plastics. Urban sewage and industrial effluents, like those in the Kosi River, contribute microplastics.

Marine Sources: Abandoned fishing gear (ALDFG) and nurdle spills from coastal petrochemical industries, like the 2023 Chennai oil spill, add to microplastic pollution, damaging mangroves and marine life.

Regional Impact

Studies by NCCR and NIO (2022-2025) show high microplastic concentrations in coastal ecosystems like mangroves and estuaries. In Goa, mangroves have 5.14 ± 0.55 plastic items/m² (66% plastic), while Maharashtra’s mangroves reach 8.5 ± 1.9 items/m² (83% plastic). Offshore sediments in the Arabian Sea also show persistent contamination, impacting benthic species critical to food chains.

Food Chain Integration

Microplastics, ingested by plankton and benthic species, bioaccumulate through the food chain, carrying toxins like BPA. Higher concentrations in benthic fish and shellfish, key to India’s $8 billion seafood industry, threaten nutrition security and increase operational costs for coastal communities.

Health Risks

Microplastics in seafood, salt, and sugar pose long-term health risks, with nanoplastics penetrating human tissues. The FSSAI’s 2024 project and MoES-EU’s 2025 initiative (₹90 crore) aim to assess these risks, but data gaps persist, necessitating precautionary measures.

Regulatory Framework

SWM Rules (2016): Despite banning waste dumping in water bodies, poor municipal enforcement allows 80% of ocean plastics from land sources.

SUP Ban (2022): The single-use plastic ban faces uneven enforcement and lacks viable alternatives.

EPR Framework (2022): Mandates recycling but overlooks primary sources like nurdle spills, requiring stricter coastal industry regulations.

Mitigation Strategies

River Interceptors: India-Germany collaborations target cities like Kanpur to curb riverine plastic flow. Innovations: Bioplastics and advanced recycling need scaling. Global Cooperation: India supports UNEA Resolution 5.2 for a 2024 plastic pollution treaty and regional efforts with Australia and Singapore.

Recommendations

To address plastic pollution, India should:

  • Prioritize Riverine Management: Mandate waste interceptors in major river cities and use FSSAI/MoES data for microplastic tracking.
  • Strengthen EPR: Enforce higher recycling targets for non-recyclable plastics and subsidize bioplastic R&D.
  • Establish Food Safety Standards: Develop FSSAI-led microplastic tolerance limits for seafood and staples.
  • Regulate Coastal Industries: Enforce IMO standards to curb nurdle spills and ALDFG.
